🎯 Today's Learning Objective

By the end of today you'll have a precise mental model of what Product Management actually is, how it differs from adjacent roles, and what the PM mandate looks like in practice — anchored in real Indian product companies including Zepto, CRED, and Razorpay.

📖 The Lesson

Let's start with the question that trips up even experienced PMs in interviews:

"What does a Product Manager actually do?"

Most answers are vague. "I work with engineers and designers." "I own the roadmap." These aren't wrong — they're dangerously incomplete. If you can't describe your job precisely, you can't do it precisely.

A PM sits at the intersection of Business, Technology, and User Experience — and their job is to find solutions that satisfy all three simultaneously.

The PM stands at the intersection of Business, Technology, and User Experience. Remove any one domain and the role collapses.

The PM Mandate: 4 Risks to Clear Before Shipping

Marty Cagan (SVPG) defines PM work through four product risks. Before shipping anything, a PM must reduce all four to acceptable levels. Miss even one — and you ship the wrong thing, to the wrong people, at the wrong time.

All 4 must be cleared before committing to build. A feature users love but kills unit economics = viability failure. A profitable feature nobody can use = usability failure.

PM vs Other Roles — The Boundaries

Understanding where PM responsibility starts and ends is as important as understanding the role itself:

Write codeEngineersPM defines what & why. Engineers decide how.

Run the projectPjM / TPMPM owns what & why. PjM owns when & how.

One-sentence summary: a PM is the CEO of the product — full accountability for outcomes, zero formal authority over the people who create them. Your only tools are clarity, trust, and evidence.

🇮🇳 Indian Case Study
🛒 Zepto — How the 4-Risk Framework Played Out

Zepto's PMs had to clear all four risks before committing to "10-minute delivery" — the boldest logistics promise in Indian e-commerce history.

✅ Value: Real JTBD — mid-recipe emergencies, infant formula runs, last-minute party prep. High-urgency moments justified a premium. The job was genuine.

✅ Viability: Only viable with dark stores at exact geographic density. PMs modelled coverage vs order density before every city expansion. Wrong density = unit economics collapse.

✅ Usability: Checkout ruthlessly simplified — fewer taps than Swiggy Instamart. Every extra screen adds latency to an "I need this NOW" state.

✅ Feasibility: Required rebuilding dark store packing, routing, and rider assignment from scratch. PMs co-designed the operations — not just the app.

Zepto cleared all 4 risks before committing. PMs co-designed the operations — not just the app.
⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes — Week 1 Edition
❌ Mistake #1 — Confusing Features with Solutions

New PMs jump to "we should build X" without asking what problem X solves. A feature is a hypothesis, not an answer. Complete this sentence first: "We're building this because users need [Y] so that [business outcome Z]." If you can't — you're not ready to build it.

❌ Mistake #2 — Thinking PM = Boss

PMs have enormous influence and zero formal authority. You can't order engineers to build something — you earn buy-in through trust, data, and conviction. New PMs who try to play CEO get ignored. The best PMs make the team feel like they made every decision.

❌ Mistake #3 — Measuring Output Not Outcome

"We shipped 14 features this quarter" is a vanity metric. "We grew day-7 retention from 38% to 51%" is what matters. PMs are always judged by outcomes — changes in user behaviour and business metrics, never by changelog length.

Valuable to users · Viable for business · Feasible to build · Usable in practice — simultaneously, always.

✅ Key Takeaways
✅
The PM mandate: find solutions simultaneously valuable to users, viable for the business, feasible for the team, and usable in practice. All four — not three.
✅
PMs influence without authority. You earn buy-in through trust, data, and shared conviction — not through seniority or orders.
✅
PMs are judged by outcomes — user behaviour changes and business metrics — never by features shipped or velocity delivered.
✅
B2C and B2B PM roles feel like different jobs. Know which world you're aiming for — it shapes every skill to prioritise over the next 6 months.
