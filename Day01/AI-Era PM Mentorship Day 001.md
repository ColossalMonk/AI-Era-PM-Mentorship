### **Repeating Patterns Across All Three JDs**

Despite Zepto focusing on logistics, CRED on premium consumer experience, and Razorpay on merchant infrastructure, **four core pillars** repeat across all three roles:

#### **1. The Strategy-to-Execution Loop**

- **The Pattern:** All three companies demand that a PM simultaneously "zoom out" and "zoom in."
- **Evidence:** Every single JD explicitly leads with **Product Strategy** as the primary responsibility, closely tied to daily, tactical delivery (e.g., *Sprint Execution* at Zepto, *Zero-Defect Execution* at CRED, and *PRD Drafting* at Razorpay).

- **The Pattern:** PMs at these firms do not work in isolation; they are the central glue holding diverse teams together.
- **Evidence:** The JDs heavily emphasize managing complex internal and external relationships (e.g., *Cross-Functional Collaboration* at Zepto, *Cross-Functional Ownership* at CRED, and *Stakeholder Management* at Razorpay).

#### **3. Ruthless Analytical Rigor**

- **The Pattern:** Decisions cannot be based on guesswork or intuition alone; every product choice must be backed by hard metrics.

- **Evidence:** This manifests as an *Entrepreneurial Mindset* at Zepto, *System Thinking* at CRED, and *Problem-First Thinking* at Razorpay.

## Write a **200-word answer** to "What does a PM do?" in plain English — family dinner, zero jargon, no "I align stakeholders."

A product manager is like the director of a movie. They don’t act in the scenes, set up the lights, or write the music, but they are completely responsible for making sure the final movie is great and people love watching it.

In a tech company, software engineers build the app and designers make it look beautiful. The product manager’s job is to figure out exactly what they should build in the first place.

To do this, they spend their days talking to users to learn about their everyday frustrations. Then, they look at facts and numbers to see if fixing that frustration makes business sense. Finally, they sit down with the builders to explain the problem clearly so everyone can work together on a smart solution.

If a new feature succeeds, the whole team gets the credit. If it fails or confuses users, it is the product manager’s job to learn why and fix it. They don’t build the engine, but they make sure the train is traveling down the right track toward a destination customers actually care about.

An exceptional Product Manager to highlight is **Nikita Bier**. He is the only PM/founder to build two separate consumer apps from scratch—**tbh** (acquired by Facebook) and **Gas** (acquired by Discord)—and scale them both to #1 in the US App Store with zero ad spend.

The three distinct actions he took exemplify the true core of the PM mandate:

#### **1. Spotting Latent Demand Through Systemic Validation**

- **The PM Mandate:** Building what users *actually* want, not what they *say* they want.
- **The Action:** Instead of relying on focus groups or theoretical market research, Bier searched for existing, highly distorted, or clumsy user behaviors online. In his newsletter, he detailed how he noticed an obscure Arabic app topping the App Store charts. He instantly reverse-engineered the core psychological drive—teenagers seeking positive social validation—and packaged it into a cleaner, localized interface.

#### **2. Ruthless Friction Reduction (The 3-Second Rule)**

- **The PM Mandate:** Optimizing user experience by ruthlessly prioritizing time-to-value.
- **The Action:** Bier established a strict design framework: if a user does not experience the "aha moment" within three seconds of opening the app, the product has failed. In his product teardowns shared via LinkedIn, he demonstrated how he stripped away multi-step signups, complex tutorials, and legacy profile configurations. Opening his apps dropped users immediately into a single, high-reward action: voting in anonymous, positive polls for their friends.

#### **3. Systematic Network Mechanics Over Marketing Budgets**

- **The PM Mandate:** Engineering organic growth directly into the product loop rather than relying on marketing.
- **The Action:** According to his tech stack deep-dives, Bier realized that targeting adults required an unsustainable Cost Per Acquisition (CPA). He instead targeted dense, high-frequency physical networks: high schools. He strategically geofenced early rollouts—launching the app in only one school at a time. By creating localized artificial scarcity and a fear of missing out (FOMO) among tight-knit student groups, he caused the app to hit a critical viral threshold of 40% local penetration within 24 hours without a single dollar spent on traditional advertising.
