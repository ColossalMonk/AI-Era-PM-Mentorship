🎯 Today's Learning Objective

📖 The Lesson

Yesterday you learned what a PM does. Today: what a PM is actually optimizing for. Every feature you'll ever propose exists inside a business model — and if you don't understand the model, you can't tell if your feature is a good idea or a slow-motion mistake.

Here's the shift in thinking: stop looking at a company as a product. Start looking at it as a system — a set of actors, connected by flows of money, data, and value, with feedback loops that either compound growth or quietly strangle it.

Every company is a system — users, product, and revenue connected in a loop. Follow the money, and the whole business model becomes obvious.

5 Business Model Archetypes

Almost every company you'll ever work at fits one of these five patterns — or a hybrid of two. Knowing which one you're in tells you instantly who your real customer is, what "success" looks like, and which metrics actually matter.

The model determines what "good" means. A marketplace PM optimizes liquidity. A SaaS PM optimizes retention. Confusing the two leads to solving the wrong problem.

Systems Thinking: The PM's Real Superpower

A causal loop diagram is the single most underused tool in product management. It answers a question metrics alone never can: why does this number move the way it does?

Two loop types matter: reinforcing loops compound — more of A creates more of B creates more of A, growth accelerates. Balancing loops self-correct — more of A eventually creates less of A, growth plateaus or reverses. Every product has both running simultaneously, often invisibly.

Reinforcing loops compound growth. Balancing loops cap it. Every metric you're trying to move lives inside one of these — find the loop before you pull the lever.

Worked example: "More riders → faster deliveries → happier customers → more orders → more riders" is reinforcing — it compounds. But "more orders → riders overloaded → slower deliveries → unhappier customers → fewer orders" is balancing, running at the same time, capping how fast the first loop can grow. A PM's job is often just: strengthen the reinforcing loop, weaken the balancing one.

Aggregation Theory — How Platforms Actually Win

Ben Thompson's Aggregation Theory explains why companies like Zomato, Amazon, and Google dominate: they sit between supply and demand and control the relationship. Supply and demand never touch each other directly — they only meet through the platform.

The middleman becomes the market. Whoever controls the connection between supply and demand controls the pricing power, the data, and the relationship.

This is why platform PMs obsess over one deceptively simple question: if supply and demand could bypass us, would they? If the answer is yes, you don't have a moat — you have a temporary convenience.

🇮🇳 Indian Case Study
🍔 Swiggy vs Zomato — Same JTBD, Diverging Systems

Both companies started solving the identical job: get food delivered fast. Same market, same timing, nearly identical early product. Five years later, their systems look completely different.

Swiggy's bet: doubled down on speed and logistics — building Instamart as a quick-commerce extension of the same delivery-fleet system they already had. One reinforcing loop, made stronger.

Zomato's bet: diversified the system entirely — Dining Out (restaurant discovery/reservations) plus acquiring Blinkit outright rather than building quick-commerce internally. Multiple loops, hedged against any single one weakening.

Neither choice was "wrong." They reflect different systems-thinking bets about where the reinforcing loops were strongest — and where the balancing loops (fleet capacity, delivery radius, restaurant supply) would bite first.

Identical starting JTBD. Completely different systems-thinking bets. Two different five-year outcomes.
⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes — Systems Thinking Edition
❌ Mistake #1 — Confusing Revenue Model with Business Model

"We charge a subscription" is a revenue model, not a business model. The business model is the entire system — who the actors are, what they exchange, and what loops sustain it. Two companies can share a revenue model and have opposite business models.

❌ Mistake #2 — Shipping Features That Ignore the Loop

A feature that boosts one metric while quietly weakening the loop that sustains it is a trap. Adding aggressive push notifications might spike short-term opens while eroding the trust loop that drives long-term retention. Always ask: which loop does this feature strengthen, and which might it damage?

❌ Mistake #3 — Treating Every User as a Paying Customer

In a marketplace, half your "users" — the supply side — are rarely who you monetize directly. Design decisions that delight demand while quietly punishing supply (like Swiggy's delivery partners) eventually collapse the whole system, revenue included.

🏋️ Day 2 Exercise
Business Model Teardown

Pick two companies in the same space with different systems — building on today's Swiggy/Zomato example, or choose your own pair.

1
Choose 2 companies solving the same core job (e.g. Swiggy vs Zomato, or PhonePe vs Paytm). Draw an actor diagram for each — who exchanges what with whom.
2
Identify 2 reinforcing feedback loops per company. Write each as a chain: "More X → More Y → More X."
3
Identify 1 balancing loop per company — the thing that caps their growth or creates friction.
4
Map all revenue streams for both companies. Which one has more diversified loops? Which is more fragile if one loop breaks?
★
Bonus: Write one paragraph — which company's system would you rather be the PM inside of, and why? Defend it with the loops you identified, not vibes.
